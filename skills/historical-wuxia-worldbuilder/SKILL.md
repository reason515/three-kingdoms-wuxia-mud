# Historical Wuxia Worldbuilder

## Purpose

Create **history seen through jianghu eyes**. Preserve recognizable historical outcomes while turning gaps in the record into playable conflicts involving couriers, scouts, physicians, refugees, retainers, merchants, sects, secret letters and personal loyalties.

## Historical adaptation model

For every scenario, separate three layers:

1. **Historical anchors**: dated events, major outcomes and known relationships that should remain recognizable.
2. **Playable gaps**: undocumented processes, motives, intelligence failures, minor operations and local consequences that players may influence.
3. **Jianghu fates**: fictional or sparsely recorded people whose survival, allegiance, reputation and legacy can change substantially.

Do not let a convenient player choice erase the weight of history. Prefer changing the hidden cause, human cost, surviving evidence or later interpretation of an event.

## Core principles

- Verify chronology, geography, offices, factions and known outcomes before adding fiction.
- Mark uncertain or disputed history explicitly; uncertainty is useful material for conflicting perspectives.
- Let wuxia inhabit historical gaps, not replace political and military logic.
- Prefer grounded martial skill, espionage and human capability over high fantasy.
- Use famous figures sparingly and preserve their agency; they are not rewards for the player to collect.
- Do not turn every historical figure into a martial arts master.
- Give each location conflict, routes, secrets, time pressure and reasons to revisit from another identity.
- Treat common people and minor officials as consequential, not disposable scenery.

## Required output

When designing a setting or historical scenario, include:

1. **Time and place**
2. **Confirmed historical anchors**
3. **Disputed or unknown areas**
4. **Player-changeable boundaries**
5. **Jianghu transformation**
6. **Factions and interests**
7. **Playable identities**
8. **Locations and routes**
9. **Secrets, rumors and evidence**
10. **Continuity and anachronism risks**
11. **Hooks for choices, relationships, abilities and endings**

## Useful conflicts

- oath versus state;
- truth versus useful fiction;
- mission versus human life;
- loyalty versus survival;
- sect reputation versus public good;
- private friendship versus faction duty;
- preserving evidence versus protecting a person.

## Avoid

- Directly copying names, organizations, plots or signature techniques from reference novels or games.
- Treating fictional conspiracies as explanations for every major historical event.
- Lore dumps without playable consequences.
- Alternate history that silently contradicts the scenario's declared anchors.
